The Great Blizzard of Los Angeles: A Once-in-a-Millennium Event (Except for That Other Time and the Time Before That)

Ah, Los Angeles. The land of eternal sunshine, bronzed bodies, and...fleeting flurries of snow? That's right, folks. Believe it or not, snow in the City of Angels, while rare enough to qualify as a mythical creature (like, say, a traffic-free freeway), does occasionally happen.

So, How Rare Are We Talking?

Let's imagine you squint really hard and hold your breath for an entire minute. That's about the likelihood of a decent snowfall in the Los Angeles basin itself. The average winter low temperatures hover in the 40s Fahrenheit, a good ten degrees shy of what snowflakes need for a pool party.

However, for those who enjoy a touch of drama with their weather (and maybe own a decent snow hat - you never know!), there are some caveats:

  • The Mountainous Exceptions: Los Angeles County is home to the majestic San Gabriel Mountains. Up there, at chillier elevations, snowfall is a regular winter visitor. So, if you're craving a snowball fight, a quick jaunt up the mountain might be in order. Just be sure to pack some sunscreen too - California weather can be a confusing beast.

  • The Not-So-Great Blizzard of '07: Every once in a blue moon (or perhaps a particularly cold one), a freak weather system throws Los Angeles a curveball. In 2007, the city experienced a dusting of snow, causing absolute pandemonium. People flocked to the streets, armed with smartphones instead of shovels, to capture this once-in-a-lifetime event (or at least, a once-every-few-decades event).

Fun Fact: Traffic came to a standstill that day, not because of the snow itself, but because everyone was too busy taking pictures of it.

So, Should You Pack Your Snowboots for Your LA Trip?

Probably not. Unless you're planning a mountain hike or are the world's biggest gambler, leave the winter gear at home. But hey, if you do get caught in a surprise flurry, you can always say you witnessed a true Los Angeles miracle. Just be sure to document it for posterity (and social media bragging rights, of course).
